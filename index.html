What are Autonomous Vehicles?

A fully autonomous vehicle (AV) uses inbuilt and external sensors to perform required operations without human involvement. The operations of a fully autonomous vehicle mimic those of a human, and do not require human intervention to perform correctly even in the most complex situations, therefore distinguishing them from mere automation (Taeihagh & Si Min Lim, 2018). The obvious difference to the current state is that autonomous vehicles do not require a human driver. The more complex design behind AVs is the complex interaction between artificial intelligence (AI), sensors and big data to analyse information, adapt to changing situations on the road (Taeihagh & Si Min Lim, 2018). The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) International in 2014 issued the SAE J3016 standard defining the full range of levels of Automated Level. It has subsequently made several revisions with the latest being revised in April 2021. Below is a summary of the automation levels from the standard (SAE International, 2021). As can be seen in the graphic there are 6 levels of autonomous vehicles, ranging from Level 0 of no driving automation, where the driver performs the entire dynamic driving task to Level 5 where the is sustained and unconditional performance by autonomous driving system of the entire dynamic driving task, with no expectation that the user will need to intervene (SAE International, 2021).

While the end goal of AV manufacturers is the same, Level 5 fully automation. Waymo uses high resolution cameras and lidar (light detection and ranging) and a way of estimating distances to objects by bouncing light off those objects (Piper, 2020). Tesla’s self-driving system uses a forward radar, a forward-looking camera, 12 long-range ultrasonic sensors placed around the car. It uses the camera to identify obstacles ahead of it, the radar detects moving objects ahead of the car including vehicles, bicycles and people, and the ultrasound sensors can sense objects in a 360-degree view around the car at any speed at up to 5 meters away, helping the car calculate any obstacles or hazards and their direction (Thompson, 2016). Most industry players use a slightly different combination of cameras and sensors for the cars to see, as there is not proven and established best practice for ways to make the cars “see”. It is clear though, that the cars will be able to visualise a full 360-degree picture around them when they are on the road, which is much more than what the human can ever perceive. One of the obstacles in developing AVs is training the AI that is used to interpret the massive volume of data that the AV collects from all its sensors. The data is multiplied by millions of magnitudes as it is collected from all the AVs and semi-AVs currently on the road and sent to data repositories to be reused in training new and improved Ais (IEEE Innovation at work, 2021). AV technology is not just for cars, it is already in use in many industries which have much more controlled environments, which have much lower unexpected situations than public roads. Some examples include Sydney Metro autonomous trains, Amazon warehouse autonomous picking systems, autonomous mining haulage systems in mines and others.

Predictions from 2016 had up to 70% of all distance being driven by autonomous vehicles by 2035, while more recent predictions are more reserved 20% of the distance driven will be by autonomous vehicles by 2040 (Anderson, 2020). These differences are so varied due to the recent challenges experienced and strategic shifts AV developers made due to the difficulty of developing a fully autonomous in any conditions Level 5 vehicle. They are now focusing on more realistic Level 4 autonomous which are available in certain geographic locations and under specific conditions (Anderson, 2020). Some of the challenges AV manufacturers experience include lack of consensus on the end goal of AV technology, lack of current data that can be utilised to train AI, lack of connectivity between vehicles of different manufacturers (Wiggers, 2020).

There are some key technologies that are behind AVs, and improvements in these technologies will get AVs closer to public roads (MIT Technology Review Insights, 2019). The application of AI is a major focus as it is a key factor in distinguishing autonomous with automated. AI needs to be the decision maker in place of humans, and such it needs to be developed enough to make the right decisions in all AV scenarios. AI market is responding to this need, with the AV AI market expected to reach $10.5bn globally by 2025 (Markets and Markets, 2017). Security is another technology that will need to keep up with the rapid development of AVs. Protection against hacking of self-driving vehicles is key to ensuring widespread take-up. It is estimated the AV cybersecurity market will grow to $5.77bn by 2025 (Markets and Markets, 2020). Machine Learning

The systematic and automatic improvement of a computer program in identifying and classifying data or items offers a wide range of opportunities in computer science (Ivanović & Radovanović, 2014). Machine learning has proliferated in its use in day-to-day life, and essential function, especially in its use in the medical fields during the onset of COVID-19 (Code Motion, 2021). The utility of machine learning has expanded from limited classification, to include areas such as natural language processing (NLP), computer vision and robotics process automation (RPA).

In the years immediately preceding 2021, we saw the introduction of Googles BERT algorithm and its utility in NLP. This algorithm, developed by a Google research team in 2018, has already replaced Googles and many other organisations algorithms for NLP in the modern context. Beyond the implications for advancement in the NLP field, the BERT algorithm stands as an example of the implications that modern machine learning have on computational ability (Code Motion, 2020).

For most previous machine learning algorithms, supervised learning was the method utilised to allow the algorithm to adjust and learn how to classify the data it received. This method, however, does not work with data sets with unknown or out of context labels, and for that unsupervised machine learning can be employed. Rather than classifying information along a pre-set list of labels, unsupervised machine learning can take blind data (rather than the required data and result for supervised learning) and self-organise a data structure that allows the algorithm to flexibly identify new, or missing context results (Wiggers, 2021).

The utility of such a data structure as given by unsupervised machine learning offer a different form to classification, clusters, and regression. By giving each element a position relative to other data, it allows the network to form associations with similar data, including that which closely resembles other information, and yet is distinct from it. This is of advantage in medical fields where the introduction of data out of context to that already known, would not allow for accurate determination of results and calculations (Sidey-Gibbons & Sidey-Gibbons, 2019).

In addition to unsupervised machine learning, deep learning is also essential to the growth of machine learning potential. Deep learning uses the model of the brain as inspiration and builds a network made from neurons named a neural network. This network can be trained with a large amount of information to give increasingly accurate labels to content. In opposition to older styles of machine learning, also called ‘flavours’, deep learning algorithms (neural nets) continue to see accuracy and performance growth with more data, rather than reaching a plateau like historic flavours. Of note also, is that although early results were noted in supervised training, the enormous amounts of data required, and the large amounts readily available from the internet and other sources allows deep learning to be suited for unsupervised learning (Code Motion, 2021). The fundamental improvements in computing technology and in computational power have single handily been the revolutionising factor in bringing an old hypothesis into fruition for the 21st century and beyond (Brownlee, 2019).

Finally, in the discussion of the state of the art of machine learning, its advancements, and the causation of these advancements, is the advent of robotics process automation (RPA). RPA deals not with actual robotics, but with the automation of repetitive tasks within a computer system, examples being rule based and trigger driven copy and paste, file transfer and conversion tasks (Casey, 2020). These tasks encompass a combination of computer vision and machine learning tools that together, provide a not altogether new system or technology. Project Idea

Overview

Our project idea will revolve around creating a video game using the Unity real-time development platform. The platform has a built-in visual scripting system, C# programming environment via Microsoft Visual Studio, and AI interfaces that can be modified using C#. With these utilities, the team will create a gaming environment to cater to the interests of the gaming community at large. To achieve this, the project will bring together ideas on artificial intelligence within a gaming environment and Non-Player Character (NPC) interactions with the player. Our aim is to add value to the gaming market by creating innovative approaches to player interactions in a gaming environment.

Motivation

The primary motivation for this project idea is to deliver a more interactive gaming experience by implementing artificial intelligence into a gaming environment, with the aim of fostering innovation within the software development industry. In this project, we will develop a game that can interact with the player in exciting ways. A key motivating factor of developing the game is that it will demonstrate how the team can use a Finite-State Machine (FSM) within a video game.

Standard artificial intelligence concepts are implemented using an FSM, a mathematical model of computation, which can be in exactly one of several states at a time. This allows the game to behave in a predictable manner. The team will first start creating the game environment and focus on making a system that can automatically generate random, yet playable map levels. By utilising Unity’s AI functionality to create thousands of map iterations that are suitable for gameplay, this will allow the team to focus more of their time on developing the rest of the game’s key components, and the visual content.

Another motivation is to make improvements to the FSM algorithm so that it can understand and adapt to the player’s gaming habits, which can make the game mechanics unpredictable and challenging. It will also show how fundamental AI interactions are the building blocks for more modern and advanced AI concepts such as real-time dynamic reactions and engagements that can deliver a more immersive experience to the player.

Description

This project idea will be a top-down 3D shooter game that will be immersive, with the aim of demonstrating how artificial intelligence techniques can be combined with mathematics and visual art. The game’s design style and aesthetic will be futuristic, with the game set in the future, and the environment located in the endless depths of space. The player will control an advanced spaceship, and AI-controlled enemy spacecraft will try to destroy the player. There are asteroids and space rocks that the player must avoid while flying to prevent damaging their spaceship. The objective of the game is to reach the safe zone that is located at the end of each map.

The front end of the game will not be challenging and there is no set difficulty. However, the game will progressively challenge the player by using dynamic difficulty settings and will adjust accordingly to enhance the player's experience. The game’s final development stage will focus on keeping the player engaged and stimulated while playing the game and adapting the gameplay style to be either slow-paced, fast paced, or immersive depending on how the player is engaging with the game.

Within the back end of the game, the visual scripting systems and FSM algorithms will challenge the player with AI-generated map levels, and unpredictable NPC interactions. The team will then use these techniques to create an innovative game.

Tools and Technologies

The project will make use of Unity’s development platform and Microsoft Visual Studio. These programs will require capable hardware to run, requiring decent minimum requirements for storage and graphics, which will be used for developing, rendering, and testing the game. Using both programs will allow the team to create, debug and deploy the finished game.

To help track changes to source code, GitHub will be used as a version control system and online hosting provider that the team will use to collaborate with each other while developing. Team members will branch off the main branch to work on changes before they are implemented into the main game. Using a cloud-based service to host the source code reduces the risk of the code being accidentally lost or destroyed and allows the team members to work on the project at any time. Members are free to clone the repository and work on their own forks locally, and then push their changes to the main branch when ready.

Team Roles

Each team member will be given a role based on their skills and experience. The team will be split up into the following roles:

  • Lead Developer
  • Programmer
  • Game Designer

There may be additional roles depending on the scope of the project. For example, the team may decide to add features or new gameplay mechanics that would require the skills and guidance of an expert in that area. While the team members may be able to work on a prototype of that new addition to the game, the team should consider outsourcing individuals, development teams, or companies.

The team is small, and so collaborating with other team members is crucial to ensure that everyone stays on track and development of the game can continue at a steady pace. Each member will contribute their feedback with one another and share ideas that can be incorporated into the game. Ideas that are not ready can be implemented into a team member’s individual fork of the game source code. If the idea gains approval from all members, then the individual changes can be merged into the main branch. Due to the size of the team, members will need to contribute to parts outside of their expertise as required.

From there, this cycle repeats and eventually the team will reach a stage where the game has had substantial work put into it. At this point, the game could be ready for an initial beta release that other users can download and test. These users can then provide feedback on what they think of the game, such as what needs improvement and what could be added, changed, or removed. Their feedback can help shape the course of development, and if enough people provide feedback or are interested in the game, this will really motivate the team to continue working on it.

Outcome

If the project idea is successful, the team will have developed and documented their experiences with AI concepts, real-time development tools, programming, and game design. This can then lead to other ideas and advances with the tools and ideas used throughout the project. The team will have developed a design document that outlines the innovative approaches used in the game design and development process, and what implementations the team used to enhance the various NPC algorithms and player experiences. The design document will also go into detail about the techniques used in the development process, and the reasons why the team chose a particular method or algorithm, how it was used in the game, and how it could be improved or adapted into other games or software products.

The primary focus of this project idea is not on generating game sales, but at improving the way video games are developed, and to provide additional value and innovation to the game industry in hopes of inspiring others to create innovative games. The project will have met its original design goal by showing that artificial intelligence can be incorporated into a game environment to produce unpredictable behaviour and change the way people interact with games. The game will therefore be a use case to showcase the application of the improved AI technology, that could be utilised in future games, or licensed to third-party development houses to include in their games/technologies. Developers can then take their experience from this project and use it to develop new and creative ways for players to interact within a game environment using artificial intelligence.
